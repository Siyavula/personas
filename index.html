Keith

Head of Science Department

EdTech Sceptic

I don't really trust Intelligent Practice. I know what the kids want to know, and can teach it to them.

Keith is the head of the science department at an exclusive single-sex, private school and has been doing so for many years. He uses technology in the classroom, to pull up examples or material on the board (much like an overhead projector). Keith's students all have laptops and/or tablets but he doesn't believe that teaching Science on a computer is a good idea. In general, he is not convinced by the edtech movement.

He feels that he is paid to stand in front of his class and teach them, and that parents at the school would not be impressed if he tried to 'replace himself' with computers in class. He doesn't want to place trust in a system that assigns work itself - he feels that "he knows what he wants the kids to know". He thinks kids are lazy, and that if they do work while connected to the internet, they'll just search for answers online or get completely distracted.

While Keith acknowledges that his school is an ideal candidate for a product like Intelligent Practice (because all the kid have devices), he feels it's not compatible with his teaching methods and that it's superfluous for such a well-resourced school.

Suzanna

Maths teacher

Low-Tech Believer

Intelligent Practice is a great idea, but there's lots of admin to do, and the site is so slow!

Suzanna teaches maths at a large former Model C (government) school. Her classroom is on the 3rd floor of the building, and she shares it with other teachers because the school is oversubscribed. If she wants to mark homework or tests, she has to do it in the staff room on the ground floor. The internet connection at her school is dismal. She can only get online in the computer lab, and even then it's really slow. The computers run Windows XP with Internet Explorer 7. Her kids have access to this lab but she has to book time in advance.

Suzanna teaches very large classes. She's had huge issues getting her kids subscribed to IP using vouchers and collecting money from their parents. Every week she battles with resetting their usernames and passwords. Sometimes she only discovers that a child has lost their login details while the kids are practising and some of them aren't making any progress on her dashboard during class. She finds it very difficult to justify spending lots of time on or IP or integrate it successfully into her classroom because not all of her kids are signed up.

Suzanna likes the practice service and loves the idea of using technology in the classroom but, by her own admission, is an impatient person. She very quickly tired of having to practice questions herself in order to view them, and gets very frustrated with how long it takes the website (books, practice, dashboard) to load. She does however, find it really useful to be able to pull up Everything Maths on her projector to show kids questions and examples in class.

Bongani

Part-time Maths tutor

StatsMan

I need to be able to catch problems during a session, so that I can talk to the kids right away.

Bongani tutors part-time at a maths centre on the edge of a large township. Next door to the centre are a welding workshop and a sewing shop. The centre has two computer labs - one is dedicated to tutoring maths for kids in the senior grades. The other lab is shared between adult computer literacy classes and sessions for the kids in junior high school grades. The centre has decent computers (15 - 20 per lab) and internet connectivity but the cost of data can be prohibitive.

The centre works on a first-come-first-serve basis (for kids from any schools) and if a kid's attendance drops below 75%, their place in the program is given to someone else.

Bongani find that learners benefit from more prescriptive and structured ways of learning, otherwise they land up practising the wrong content. He needs live diagnostics on a learner-by-learner basis, not just for a class, so that he can catch problems as and when they arise during a tutorial session.

Literacy and computer literacy levels amongst the kids are low, so while the focus is on maths tutoring, Bongani also lands up helping kids improve their literacy, numeracy and computer skills during tutorials.

Roger

Science teacher

Intelligent Practice SuperFan

I love using the Intelligent Practice Dashboards and summaries to see how my students are doing, and to show them how they are doing.

Roger teaches science at a massively-resourced private school. Roger is hugely enthusiastic about technology and has successfully integrated it into his classroom and his teaching. All of his kids have access to laptops or tablets. Roger believes in encouraging his kids to take responsibility for their own learning - inside and outside of the classroom. Roger is a highly-skilled and very busy teacher, who is very enthusiastic about his subject and his career.

Roger is a huge fan of Intelligent Practice and largely trusts the system, despite having encountered a few bugs. His kids have been frustrated by the learner experience on IP, but he still believes it adds value, and incorporates it as a small part of their class assessment. He uses the learner dashboard in class to show kids what they need to practice. He also pulls up the teacher dashboard to show his class how they're progressing. He finds the number of exercises and mastery vs speed plots, and the exercises summary on the teacher dash particularly useful. He would like his kids to have access to this information too. He uses these plots to get, at a glance, a sense of how much effort his kids have put in on the system, and how well they're working.

Skhumbuzo

I use Intelligent Practice - it's saving my ass in Physics!

Skhu, as he goes by among friends and teachers, is in Gr 10 and attends an all-boys school in Grahamstown. He is an all-round achiever in the classroom and on the sports field. Skhu is particularly conscientious about his homework and doesn't like to leave things until the last minute.

Skhu steadily obtains results in the 70's, which he is happy with, although his parents would like him to move up to an A. Skhu and his friends love technology - he has his own laptop and is hoping for a tablet soon. He uses Intelligent Practice regularly, normally on his own laptop in his room in the boarding house, or sometimes in the school lab when his teacher has organised extra lessons for small groups in the afternoon. He will be going to university, but hasn't quite decided where yet.

Andile

Maths is important because it can open a lot of opportunities for me and my family. I just wish is wasn't so hard.

Andile is in Gr 10 and attends the local high school in Worcester, which he can get to by a short 2km walk and then catching the bus. He is currently getting marks in the range of 45-50%, but they fluctuate a lot, depending on external and internal influences.

Andile is in a big class, and although their teacher tries her best, he feels resentful and frustrated with his circumstances. When he gets the chance to use his older sister's feature phone, he tries to practise on Intelligent Practice, but finds it challenging. Andile sometimes stays a bit later at school to use the computer lab, when it is not locked, as he also wants to develop his computer skills. Andile wants a better life for himself and his family, but is finding it difficult to maintain his determination and motivation. He really wants to study further after school.
